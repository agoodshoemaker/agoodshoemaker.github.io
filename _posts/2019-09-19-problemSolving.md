Mathematics at the high school level doesn't have a lot of theory. There is almost an exclusive emphasis on problem solving. Few essential concepts are introduced at the beginning of a new topic and subsequent classes are devoted to solving problems based on these concepts. A year of high school typically covers six to seven such topics. There may be exceptions to this trend, but high school mathematics is usually about problem solving. <br><br>


If problem solving is the centerpiece in mathematics education at schools, what are teachers expected to teach? Before we try to answer this question, let us pause for a moment and see what happens in a classroom on some given day. Let us say the class is discussing algebraic identities. If the word *discussing* sounds too farfetched, please correct it to something less democratic. The students are familiar with most of the important identities that the teacher has covered in previous classes. Among the many problems that they are working on, the teacher writes the following one on the board:


$$
(x^4 + 1)(x^2 + 1)(x + 1)(x^8 + 1)(x^{16} + 1) = \cfrac{f(x)}{x - 1}
$$

She asks the students to find the polynomial $f(x)$ and gives them two minutes to think about the problem. With no one coming up with any ideas, she writes down the solution, explaining the steps involved in the process:
<br><br>


1) First rearrange the binomials on the left in ascending order of power:

   $$
   (x + 1)(x^2 + 1)(x^4 + 1)(x^8 + 1)(x^{16} + 1) = \cfrac{f(x)}{x - 1}
   $$

<br><br>

2) Multiply both sides by $(x - 1)$:

   $$
   (x - 1)(x + 1)(x^2 + 1)(x^4 + 1)(x^8 + 1)(x^{16} + 1) = f(x)
   $$

<br><br>

3) Make use of the identity $(a - b)(a + b) = a^2 - b^2$ repeatedly to get:

   $$
   (x^{32} - 1) = f(x)
   $$
   
<br><br>

From the teacher's perspective it is a job well done. She has written down a logically clear solution to the problem which her students can now copy. If some of them fail to understand a step or two, she can always go over them again. Were this question to appear in an exam, she can be assured that her students will write the solution without breaking a sweat. Her success is measured by the student's success in clearing examinations. <br><br>

From the student's perspective it is one more question for which he knows the correct answer. Again, if this question comes up in an exam, he can write down the solution as lucidly as it was presented to him on the blackboard. If we are a bit idealistic, we can hope that the student appreciates the interesting pattern that forms the basis of this question. <br><br>

Back to the pedagogical question with which we started, what are teachers expected to teach? From what happens in classrooms we could say that a mathematics teacher writes down the solution to mathematical problems. If that sounds too mechanical and dry, we could say that she teaches students how to solve problems in mathematics. Let us first see what we mean by solving a mathematical problem. There are two stages to it: <br><br>

1) Insight

<br><br>

2) Computation

<br><br>

The first few years of education trains the student to become fluent in mathematical computation: basic operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication and division of integers, manipulating fractions, solving simple equations and so on. Even post that a lot of what students do can only be called exercises in computation. Any problem for which the procedure or algorithm is clearly known to the student beforehand becomes an exercise in computation for him. He has to be a human calculator for the duration of solving that problem. A simple example of such a problem is given below:

$$
2x + 3y = 10\\
7x - 4y = 20
$$

We have to solve for $x, y$. I am not suggesting that this is a bad problem. On the contrary, if the student is still unclear about the procedure for solving a system of linear equations in two variables, by all means make him solve as many problems as are necessary until he groks the algorithm. But once he has got the hang of it, every additional problem of the same type will be only be a burden. The student reaches a point where fluency in algorithmic procedures starts mattering less and less. The more problems that he is given which come from a problem type that has been previously encountered, the more he becomes a human computer, and less a thinker. <br><br>

 
The soul of a problem is the insight that gave birth to it. Computation is merely the outer covering. Another analogy can be drawn from the game of basketball. In performing a dunk, a player first needs to jump high enough so that his arms extend beyond the basket. And then it is about bringing his arm down forcefully over the hoop. An insight is that jump or big leap from the ground. Computation is about completing the dunk by bringing the ball down. In the problem that we just solved, there were two insights: the first was about rearranging the binomial terms in ascending order of degree, the second was to sense the possibility of using the identity $(a - b)(a + b)$ in the first place. The rest is the job of computation where the student repeatedly applies the identity to get the final answer.<br><br>

Now consider what happens if the teacher just gives the student the insight without giving him sufficient time to think about it. The student just has to compute. This is akin to someone lifting you on the basketball court high enough so that you can put the ball through the hoop. While you may be greatful for the help now, remember that there will be no one around tomorrow to lift you up. We have a paradox here. The job of the teacher is to teach problem solving which involves two components: insight and computation. We have already established that mere computation is bound to get boring for the students at some stage. If she communicates the insight to them then the task of problem solving reduces to one of computation. One way to get around this problem is for the teacher to assume the role of a writer of detective stories. She cannot reveal the entire plot right away. She needs to gradually uncover it. This is alright for problems that involve lot of insights. But when there is just one key insight, what could she do? <br><br>


The paradox disappears if we reformulate the meaning of teaching for problem solving. For that we need to look at insights more closely. Insights come in two types: original and borrowed. By an original insight I mean a mathematical thought that the student discovers while solving a problem without any external aid. An original insight don't mean something that has never been thought by anyone before. That is not possible, especially in mathematics, and that too for such young minds. In all likelihood, the student's solution to the problem will be just the same as the one given in the back of the textbook. Originality in this context is closer to a personal discovery that is only reliant on the devices of the student's memory and intuition. Maybe he realizes that this problem looks similar to a part of some problem he solved many days back. He suddenly discovers some pattern that had eluded him an hour back. It becomes clear that an original insight cannot be communicated by the teacher. In schools students do computations on borrowed insights in the name of problem solving.  <br><br>

The conclusion that we have to arrive at is that problem solving cannot be taught. Problem solving is about performing computations on top of original insights. It is an unfortunate fact that most students finish their schooling by learning to become fluent in performing computations on borrowed insights. They have become good computers with unoriginal minds. The data is copied from an external hard drive whom we call a teacher. Why did things become like this. A lot of it has to do with examinations and standardized testing. Public exams are common to all students. Conducting an exam on the same day across the country is no small feat. This introduces some obvious constraints. Most of the questions will be drawn from a pre-specified pool of problem-templates. There will be hardly any questions that require original insight. A good human computer would know the procedure to solve every problem in the exam.<br><br>


Does this mean that teachers are useless and students can do everything on their own. Of course not! Teachers have several important responsibilities: <br><br>

1) Organization of problems with increasing levels of difficulty <br><br>

2) Preparation and presentation of problems in the right sequence that is customized for each student <br><br>

3) Teaching strategies for problem solving <br><br>

4) Invigilating problem solving sessions where students silently work on problems individually <br><br>

5) Offering help when called for and pointing errors and misconceptions <br><br>

6) Conducting discussion sessions with students for solutions to difficult problems <br><br>


There could be several innovative methods here. Discussions sessions could be modeled after Russian competitions. Let us say that a worksheet has six problems and the students are given two days to solve the problems. In the competition, two students face off against each other. One student, called the challenger, can challenge the other to provide the answer to a question of his choice. The challenger can then interrogate his opponent on the proposed solution. If he is unable to find any faults then the challegner's opponent wins. If he manages to find significant problems with the solution then the challenger wins. This will expose every solution to extreme levels of scrutiny. The teacher could also take up the role of the challegner.<br><br>

Finally, the teacher should realize that time is the greatest teacher. There can be no substitute for it. If a student spends four hours sitting with a problem trying to figure out the solution then his perseverance should be encouraged. Getting stuck is an unavoidable component of problem solving. The sooner students experience this the better it is for them. Problem solving is about thinking. It is not about knowing the correct answer. If the correct answer is always going to be provided by a third person, what joy can there be in just knowing the solution?
