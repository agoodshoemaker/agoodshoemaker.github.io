Four circles of equal radius are packed into a unit square in the following manner:

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-56/i1.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

Prove that the area trapped between the circles is one-third of the area trapped between the circles and the squares.

<!--more-->

<hr><br>

We can make use of symmetry in this problem. Construct the common tangents to the four circles, shown here in dotted lines.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-56/i2.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

This divides the square into four identical smaller squares, each having a circle inscribed in it. The area between the circle and the square circumscribing it is again made up of four congruent parts. Out of these four parts, exactly one of them (lower-right corner of the upper-left square) is trapped between other circles. The other three are areas between the sides of the square and the circle. So one-third of the area lying outside the circles and between the squares is equal to the area trapped between the circles.
