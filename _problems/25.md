<b>Problem</b>: Consider the following system of linear equations:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_1 x + b_1 y &= c_1\\
a_2 x + b_2 y &= c_2
\end{align*}
$$

Find the conditions under which the system will have:<br><br>

$(1)\ \ \ $ a unique solution<br><br>
$(2)\ \ \ $ no solution<br><br>
$(3)\ \ \ $ infinitely many solutions


<!--more-->

<hr>

<b>Solution</b>:<br><br>

- We shall attempt to solve the system. This will lead us to the desired conditions. To begin with, let us assume that all coefficients are non-zero, i.e., $a_1a_2b_1b_2c_1c_2 \neq 0$.
- Now, multiply the first equation by $a_2$ and the second equation by $a_1$:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_1 a_2 x + b_1 a_2 y &= c_1 a_2\\
a_1 a_2 x + b_2 a_1 y &= c_2 a_1
\end{align*}
$$

- Subtract the two equations:

$$
\begin{align}
(b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1) y &= c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1
\end{align}
$$

- Likewise, we can multiply the first equation by $b_2$ and the second by $b_1$:

$$
\begin{align*}
b_2 a_1 x + b_1 b_2 y &= c_1 b_2\\
b_1 a_2 x + b_1 b_2 y &= c_2 b_1
\end{align*}
$$

- And subtract:

$$
\begin{align}
(b_2 a_1 - b_1 a_2) x &= c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1
\end{align}
$$

- Let us write down these two reduced equations together. Henceforth, we will call this the <b>reduced equations</b>, and the system we started with shall be called the <b>original equations</b>:

$$
\begin{align*}
(b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1) y &= c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1\ \ \ \ \ -(1)\\
(b_2 a_1 - b_1 a_2) x &= c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1\ \ \ \ \ \ -(2)
\end{align*}
$$

- To solve for $x$ and $y$, we have to divide both sides by $b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1$ in either equation. This is only possible if this term is non-zero. What happens if it is zero? The LHS of both equations will vanish irrespective of the value taken by $x$ or $y$. From here we have two possibilities, either the RHS of both equations is zero, or the RHS is non-zero. Let us consider each of these two cases:<br><br>

<b>Case-1</b>

- We have $b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1 = 0$ and $c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1 = 0$. This would result in the following condition:

$$
\cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2} = \cfrac{c_1}{c_2}
$$

- Because of this equality, the RHS of the second equation, $c_1b_2 - c_2b_1$ is also zero. Therefore, we have both equations in the reduced system of the form $0 = 0$. So any value of $(x, y) = (\alpha, \beta)$ in the XY plane seems to satisfy this equation. The entire plane is the solution! Something is obviously wrong in our analysis. Geometrically, two straight lines cannot suddenly enlarge into the entire plane. We will return to this point at the end. For now, we can take an alternative route to settle this issue:

$$
\begin{align*}
&\cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2} = \cfrac{c_1}{c_2} = k\\
\implies &a_1 = k a_2,\ b_1 = kb_2,\ c_1 = kc_2
\end{align*}
$$

- This transforms the original system of equations into:


$$
\begin{align*}
k a_2 x + k b_2 y &= k c_2\\
a_2 x + b_2 y &= c_2
\end{align*}
$$

- Eliminating $k$ from the first equation, we are left with just one equation of the form:

$$
a_2 x + b_2 y = c_2
$$

- This equation has infinite solutions. For every value of $x = \alpha$, there is a corresponding value of $y = \beta$ such that the pair $(\alpha, \beta)$ will be a solution to this equation. And this $(\alpha, \beta)$ will correspond to a point that lies on the line $a_2x + b_2 y = c_2$. Therefore, the entire plane is not the solution, but only points on a line.

- Geometrically this is the case of two lines which are coincident, hence both having the same equation. They will look as follows:

<b>Case-2</b>

- We have $b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1 = 0$ and $c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1 \neq 0$. This would result in the following condition:

$$
\cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2} \neq \cfrac{c_1}{c_2}
$$

- We can see that if the RHS of one equation is non-zero, then the RHS of the other equation is also non-zero. In fact, the RHS of both equations are either both zero or both non-zero. It can never happen that one of them is zero and the other is non-zero.
- We are left with two equations of the form $0 = \lambda$, where $\lambda \neq 0$, which is an absurdity. To better understand what is happening, let us assume the following:

$$
\begin{align*}
&\cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2}\\
\implies &a_1 = k a_2,\ b_1 = kb_2
\end{align*}
$$

- This transforms the original system of equations into:


$$
\begin{align*}
k a_2 x + k b_2 y &= c_1\\
a_2 x + b_2 y &= c_2
\end{align*}
$$

- Simplifying the first equation, we end up with:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_2 x + b_2 y &= \cfrac{c_1}{k}\\
a_2 x + b_2 y &= c_2
\end{align*}
$$

- As $\cfrac{c_1}{c_2} \neq k$, every $(\alpha, \beta)$ that satisfies the first equation will not satisfy the second and vice versa. Therefore, the system of equations has no solution under these conditions.<br><br>

- Geometrically, this will look like two parallel lines that are separated from each other by a finite, non-zero distance. We can arrive at this conclusion by looking at the slope of either equation. It is the same: $\cfrac{-a_2}{b_2}$.

<b>Case-3</b>

- Finally, we come to the only remaining possibility. Let us get back to the reduced equations that we started with:

$$
\begin{align*}
(b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1) y &= c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1\ \ \ \ \ -(1)\\
(b_2 a_1 - b_1 a_2) x &= c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1\ \ \ \ \ \ -(2)
\end{align*}
$$

- What happens when $b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1 \neq 0$? This term being zero precipitated the earlier two cases. When this is non-zero, we can comfortably divide both sides by it:

$$
\begin{align*}
y &= \cfrac{c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1}{b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1}\\
x &= \cfrac{c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1}{b_2 a_1 - b_1 a_2}
\end{align*}
$$

- The above pair is the solution for the reduced equations. Can we conclude that it will be the solutions for the original system as well? For now we will go with this claim. It will be proved in the last section.
- So we have exactly one solution in this case. Geometrically it would look as follows:

<b>Notes</b>

- Though we have completed the analysis, there are some subtle points which we have glossed over. For instance, in case-1, we obtained the solution to the reduced equation to be any pair $(x, y)$ in the plane. Let us look at the reduced equations once more:

$$
\begin{align*}
(b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1) y &= c_1 a_2 - c_2 a_1\ \ \ \ \ -(1)\\
(b_2 a_1 - b_1 a_2) x &= c_1 b_2 - c_2 b_1\ \ \ \ \ \ -(2)
\end{align*}
$$

- Notice that any pair $(x, y) = (\alpha, \beta)$ does satisfy both the equations. But we also know that only those points on the straight line $a_2 x + b_2 y = c_2$ are solutions to the original system. So where is the discrepancy? If $(\alpha, \beta)$ is a solution to the reduced system, should it not also satisfy the original system? The answer is an emphatic no! 
- Let the original system of equations be represented as follows:

$$
\begin{align*}
f_1(x, y) &= 0\\
f_2(x, y) &= 0
\end{align*}
$$

- To get the reduced system, we perform the following transformations:

$$
\begin{align*}
g_1(x, y) &= a_2 f_1(x, y) - a_1 f_2(x, y)\\
g_2(x, y) &= b_2 f_1(x, y) - b_1 f_2(x, y)
\end{align*}
$$

- We now express the reduced system as follows:

$$
\begin{align*}
g_1(x, y) &= 0\\
g_2(x, y) &= 0
\end{align*}
$$

- There are two directions which we need to be aware of:
	- original $\rightarrow$ reduced
	- reduced $\rightarrow$ original

- We shall consider each of these two directions.

<b>Original $\rightarrow$ Reduced

- Now, let $(\alpha, \beta)$ be a solution of the original system. This implies:

$$
\begin{align*}
f_1(\alpha, \beta) &= 0\\
f_2(\alpha, \beta) &= 0
\end{align*}
$$

- We see that $(\alpha, \beta)$ also satisfies the reduced system because:

$$
\begin{align*}
g_1(\alpha, \beta) &= a_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - a_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0\\
g_2(\alpha, \beta) &= b_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - b_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0
\end{align*}
$$

- What this is means is that, every solution of the original system is a solution of the reduced system. So the set of solutions of the original system is a subset of the set of solutions of the reduced system. This may not seem to be all that surprising. Now for the other direction.

<b> Reduced $\rightarrow$ Original</b>

- Let $(\alpha, \beta)$ be a solution of the reduced system. We have:

$$
\begin{align*}
g_1(\alpha, \beta) &= a_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - a_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0\\
g_2(\alpha, \beta) &= b_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - b_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0
\end{align*}
$$

- From this it follows that:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - a_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0\\
b_2 f_1(\alpha, \beta) - b_1 f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0
\end{align*}
$$

- From here it does <em>not</em> follow that $(\alpha, \beta)$ solves the original system. Now, let us integrate this information with the three cases that we solved. Let us take case-3, in which $\cfrac{a_1}{a_2} \neq \cfrac{b_1}{b_2}$. We saw that there was exactly one solution to the reduced system. Call that solution $(\alpha, \beta)$. Let us assume for the sake of argument that $f_1(\alpha, \beta) \neq 0$ and $f_2(\alpha, \beta) \neq 0$. Then using the above equations, we have:

$$
\begin{align*}
\cfrac{f_1(\alpha, \beta)}{f_2(\alpha, \beta)} = \cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2}
\end{align*}
$$

- This is a contradiction! Therefore, $(\alpha, \beta)$ also solves the original system.
- If we take up case-1, then the solution space of the reduced system was the entire plane. If $(\alpha, \beta)$ is a point in the plane that solves the original system, then it lies on the line $f_1(\alpha, \beta) = 0$, which is same as the line $f_2(\alpha, \beta) = 0$. On the other hand, if $(\alpha, \beta)$ doesn't solve the original system, we have:

$$
\begin{align*}
\cfrac{f_1(\alpha, \beta)}{f_2(\alpha, \beta)} = \cfrac{a_1}{a_2} = \cfrac{b_1}{b_2}
\end{align*}
$$

- This is indeed true. It is a mere restatement of the geometric fact that the two lines are coincident.
- So the main lesson for us here is that the direction matters, especially so when we move from the reduced to the original.
