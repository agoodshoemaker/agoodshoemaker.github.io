**(BMO-1-2014)**

In the acute-angled triangle $ABC$, the foot of the perpendicular from $B$ to $CA$ is $E$. Let $l$ be the tangent to the circle $ABC$ at $B$. Let $F$ be the foot of the perpendicular from $C$ to $l$. Prove that $EF$ is parallel to $AB$.

<!--more-->
<hr><br>

As usual, let us begin by constructing the figure with ruler-compass. 

- Whenever we have a circumcircle, it is always easy to draw the circle first. In this case we also have a tangent. It is convenient to orient the tangent parallel to one of the coordinate axes, especially if we have lines perpendicular to the tangent.

	<br>
</figure>

- Now that we have the vertex $B$, we need to choose an arbitrary point on the circumference and mark it as $A$.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i1.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- As $ABC$ is acute, the range of locations for the vertex $C$ is limited.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i2.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- Now let us go ahead and drop perpendiculars onto $AC$ and the tangent. 

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i3.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- Now join $EF$. With this we have completed the figure corresponding to the question.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i4.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- First observation is that the chord $AB$ makes an angle with respect to the tangent which is equal to the angle subtended by it in the circumference. We have marked these equal angles in red.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i5.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- Next, observe that the presence of two right angles means that $EBFC$ is a cyclic quadrilateral.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/problems/problem-64/i6.svg" width="85%" height="85%" />
	<br>
</figure>

- In the newly drawn circle, chord $EB$ makes equal angles, both of which are marked in red. We can see that both $AB$ and $EF$ make the same angles with the tangent and are hence parallel.
