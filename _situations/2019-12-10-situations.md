## Mathematical pattens

Mathematics is the study of abstract patterns. What is a pattern? It is something which has a structure and is not random. For example, draw a semicircle and take any point on its circumference. Now, if you join this point to the endpoints of the diameter with two line segments, then the angle between them is a right angle. It is not just true for the particular radius we have chosen, but is valid for any arbitrary radius. Moreover, the statement is completely independent of the point chosen on the circumference. This is a surprising pattern!

An example of an arithmetic pattern is this eight-digit number: $9494949494$. A nice observation about this number is that it is a multiple of $94$:

$$
94949494 = 1010101 \times 94
$$
 
The feature that makes this pattern interesting is "repetition". Something which may not be a pattern as far as this specific feature is concerned is this eight-digit number: $18356951$. But this happens to be a prime number. So if the feature we are interested in is "primeness" rather than "repetition" then $18356951$ counts as a relevant object in the study of the patterns associated with prime numbers. And for the record, the idea of a prime number is itself a pattern - aloof numbers that cannot be factored into any smaller numbers other than $1$ and the number itself.<br><br>

These are some beautiful patterns in elementary mathematics. Who discovered these patterns? Great mathematicians of the past. So what do mathematicians of the present do? They discover far more advanced and abstract patterns. But the aspect of discovery is common to both. This essay will try to discuss the importance of discovery in mathematics education, the role it plays in current pedagogy and the possibilities of incorporating it in a future pedagogy of mathematics.

## Making room for discovery in mathematical pedagogy

Right through high school and undergraduate education, the study of mathematics often gives one the impression that mathematical ideas have been there for ever. This may give one the feeling that mathematics is a static body of knowledge handed down from teacher to student which has to be committed to one's memory. But this is a narrow perception. Mathematics has been around for more than 2000 years, yet it has had its own process of development. What we study now is the result of discoveries made at different times in the past by mathematicians from different parts of the world. Ubiquitous mathematical ideas such as an equation, the number zero or derivatives that we take for granted today were at one point of time great discoveries and remarkable achievements of the human intellect. Our apparent ease at dealing with them doesn't diminish their greatness. <br><br>

Unfortunately, this side of mathematics is never emphasized in schools. Mathematics is a social endeavor and is practiced by humans like you and me. Mathematicians read the works of their colleagues, collaborate with them on projects, draw inspiration from their work, and discover novel ideas and new interpretations. The process of discovery is at the heart of mathematics and forms an essential component of its practice. In fact that is a mathematician's primary job: discovering new mathematical ideas. A sound mathematics education must therefore make some attempt to incorporate discovery into its pedagogical framework. Current pedagogy throws away the dynamic labor of discovery while retaining its fruit. Future pedagogy must occupy itself with the labor of discovery and reap both the fruit and the joy of discovery as rewards.<br><br>

Another reason for this static perception of mathematics is the nature of the subject itself. Once a theorem has been proved and is accepted by the community, its truth value is fixed for eternity. Other proofs may be discovered, but none of them can contradict the truth of the theorem. This is not the case with the sciences. Theories in physics can always be proven to be incomplete, the most recent example being Newton's law of Gravitation. The best way to get out of this narrow perception is to rediscover mathematical ideas in the classroom. When this path is taken, mathematics is no longer a static body of ideas, but a dynamic field of patterns that is open to exploration and discovery. Another way to widen the outlook is to discuss the history of mathematics and the life of mathematicians in classrooms. At present, such a discussion is nonexistent.


## Problems with the current pedagogy

The dominant theme of current pedagogical approaches in mathematics education is to teach students a smattering of formulas and important results, then bombard them with problems that demonstrate the application of these formulas and results. Typical topics that use this approach are mensuration and algebra, to name a few. The quadratic formula is introduced, often without any hint why the formula works in the first place, and a dozen "numericals" are solved to demonstrate the use of this formula. Or the formula for the surface area of a cube is given, and several problems discuss how this formula is applied. Though the focus is on problem solving, there is a lack of variety in problem-types and usually only one solution is presented. There is a paucity of both problem-types and solution-types. As a result, the students end up learning a mapping from problem-type to solution-type. They are not solving problems, but are essentially matching solution-types to problem-types. <br><br>

The other problem is the undue importance given to examinations. Most schools prepare students for the examination. Problems in exams never go beyond what is there in the prescribed textbook. So K-12 education becomes a routine process of "match the following" year after year. "If this problem comes up in the exam, this formula has to be used." So goes the thought of students. This wouldn't be an issue if there is richness and variety in the problem-types. But thanks to the idea of public examinations being accessible to all students, this never happens. Everything is predetermined. There is a small pool of ideas, a question-bank, from which problems will be sourced. Mathematics becomes for them an endless, but narrow stream of problem and their rigid solutions.<br><br>

And the final blow is dealt when the teacher thinks that she has done a good job when a problem and its solution has been understood by her pupils. A mathematics teacher is seen as the solution manual to a book of problems. Students rarely get time to search for a solution to a problem. There is simply no time to carry out that all-important exercise in 40 minutes of classroom-time. The teacher ends up writing down the solution even before the students have thought what it could be. This kills the last place where students can experience the process of discovery: searching for a solution. Isn't every problem like an adventure that beckons you to find that missing piece of the puzzle which will make everything clear? But the search for a solution needs time, and classrooms don't have a lot of it, especially given the way schools structure the periods in a day. On top of all this is a school's insistence on "completing the syllabus". The more problems are solved in a class, the better is a teacher's rating.

## What is a discovery?

Discovering a mathematical pattern is not the same as solving a routine problem from a school textbook. It is an intellectual *and* emotional experience. After all, a mathematical discovery is like any other discovery, a revelation of some surprising truth that is hidden from our superficial sight. And if this discovery is the result of a personal effort, however simple the discovered pattern maybe, it gives us a joy and rewards us with a sense of accomplishment. How can we help students experience this joy? Is there a way to lead them to a discovery with minimal intervention from the teacher?<br><br>

To answer these questions we need to first understand what discovery means in the context of mathematics. What is there to discover in elementary mathematics, a subject that is more than 2000 years old, and has been studied by the greatest minds in history? Absolutely nothing ground-breaking or new, but it has all the features of a discovery. This can be best illustrated by giving examples. We will go through quite a few of them in this essay.<br><br>

While solving a problem in geometry, I came across a Pythagorean triplet, namely, $(9, 40, 41)$. The closeness of one of the legs to the hypotenuse was mildly surprising: $41 - 40 = 1$. Until then, the only triplet that I had known satisfying this property was $(5, 12, 13)$. Of course there is $(3, 4, 5)$. But that somehow doesn't seem as surprising as $(5, 12, 13)$ or $(9, 40, 41)$, probably because we are so used to seeing it everywhere. Usually we can generate more triplets by multiplying the simpler ones by a constant factor. For example, from $(5, 12, 13)$ we can generate an infinite number of triplets of the form $(5k, 12k, 13k)$. But that would increase the difference between the hypotenuse and the legs, the difference would no longer be $1$, a pattern that first caught my attention. This led me to ask the question: are there other integer triplets similar in structure to these two? If so, how many are there? <br><br>

To answer this I considered the three numbers to be $(a,\ b,\ b + 1)$. Using the Pythagoras theorem:

$$
\begin{align*}
a^2 + b^2 &= (b + 1)^2\\
a^2 &= 2b + 1\\
b &= \cfrac{a^2 - 1}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

We can then choose the triplet, $\Bigg(a, \cfrac{a^2 - 1}{2}, \cfrac{a^2 + 1}{2}\Bigg)$. But we also have another condition that the triplet should consist of integers, which forces $a$ to be odd. Using this additional information, let $a = 2k - 1$, for any natural number $k$. Then the triplet takes this form:

$$
(2k - 1,\ 2k^2 - 2k,\ 2k^2 - 2k + 1)
$$

We again seem to have a problem when $k = 1$. Here the triplet becomes $(1, 0, 1)$. So we need to enforce the additional constraint that:

$$
\begin{align*}
2k^2 - 2k &> 0\\
\implies k(k - 1) &> 0\\
\implies k &> 1
\end{align*}
$$

As $k$ is a natural number, we have $k \geq 2$. Summing up, the set of integer Pythagorean triplets where the length of the hypotenuse is one more than one of its legs is given by:

> $$
  \Big\{\  (2k - 1,\  2k^2 - 2k,\  2k^2 - 2k + 1)\ \  |\ k \geq 2 \text{ and } k \in \mathbb{N}\  \Big\}
  $$

Clearly, this set is infinite. That answers our question! In the process we have discovered an interesting result about integer Pythagorean triplets. But the nature of discoveries is that one question leads to another. If we probe the triplet further, it seems to have another nice pattern:

$$
\Bigg(\ (k - 1) + k,\ \ 2 \times (k - 1) \times k,\ \ 2 \times (k - 1) \times k + 1\ \Bigg) 
$$

This can be expressed in words as follows:

> Take any two consecutive integers, both greater than 1, and compute three quantities: their sum, two times their product and one more than two times their product. These three quantities form the sides of a right triangle.

The product of consecutive numbers makes me go to triangular numbers. If $T_k$ is the $k^{th}$ triangular number, then we have the following identity for all $k \geq 2$:

> $$
  (T_k - T_{k - 2})^2 + (4 T_{k - 1})^2 = (4 T_{k - 1} + 1)^2,\ \ \ k \geq 2
 $$

This set also has another interesting property. If we take the first element of each triplet, that gives us a number whose square can be expressed as the difference of the squares of two consecutive natural numbers. So if we collect the first element of each triplet in the set, that will give us a set of natural numbers whose squares can be expressed as the difference of the squares of two consecutive natural numbers. Now, this results in a natural question. Does this set contain every natural number whose square can be expressed as the difference of two consecutive squares? Yes, it does, as we started with $a^2 = (b + 1)^2 - b^2$, which is just a rearrangement of the original equation. Along with the observation that the first element is odd, we have:

> $S_1$: set of all integer Pythagorean triplets for which the hypotenuse exceeds one of the legs by unity. <br><br>
$S_2$: set of all natural numbers whose squares can be expressed as the difference of the squares of two consecutive natural numbers.<br><br>
$S_3$: the set of odd natural numbers<br><br>
There exist **bijective mappings** between any two of these three sets. Or the **cardinality** of all three sets is the same. So we can say that there are as many integer Pythagorean triplets with the said property as there are odd numbers.

Now, none of these are new. They will not count as novel additions to the literature of problems in elementary mathematics. But the stress is not on the problem or even its solution, but on how the problem was *discovered* and how its solution was *discovered* and how the solution gave rise to more such *discoveries*. Now we shall look at a framework for understanding what is happening here.<br><br>

## Situations

I found myself in a situation that triggered a question in my mind. This question is a puzzle I am trying to solve, not one given by my teacher, but one that I discovered. So there is the first instance of discovery: bumping into something intriguing. Now I want to solve this puzzle, find an answer to the question. And so I start searching for one. After some effort, I finally succeed in finding an answer; and how wonderful is the feeling! This is the second instance of discovery. There is something about the situation that is still intriguing. Perhaps the answer to this question triggers more questions. In the process of formulating these questions and answering them, I have discovered interesting mathematical patterns. The moment that was chiefly responsible for triggering these questions is what I term a "mathematical situation". I am not overloading the word situation with some deep and profound meaning. It is very similar to the prompts that are given during creative writing contests. For example, consider this prompt:

> A man finds himself abandoned in an island.

With this prompt you could direct a movie like Cast Away with Tom Hanks as the lead. A mathematical situation is one such prompt, but instead of making movies, you make abstract patterns out of them. The framework is captured in this flowchart:

> **The process of discovery** <br><br> 
A mathematical **situation** is a collection of mathematical symbols and words which has the potential to trigger **questions** of a mathematical nature in the mind of a student. The student then beings to **search** for a solution to these questions. Once a **solution** has been found, he returns to the situation and completes one simple cycle. A more complex cycle is where something in the search process triggers another question; in this case even before a cycle is completed, a new question is waiting in the queue to be answered. At the end of a cycle, it is quite possible for the solution to hold triggers for more questions. These possibilities are represented by the additional arrows in the diagram.

How can a teacher use the above framework to help students discover patterns with minimal interference from her? Think about what a teacher would have to do:<br><br> 

She writes down two integer triplets on the board:

$$
(5, 12, 13)\ \ \text{ and } \ \ (9, 40, 41)
$$

She doesn't say anything about what she has written and just moves away after writing this. After a while, she asks them to think about what is there on the board and gives the students some time. Then the class opens for discussion. Now a student looking at the triplets for the first time may have his attention drawn to $(5, 12, 13)$, a familiar Pythagorean triplet. This triggers a question in him, is the other one also a Pythagorean triplet? He will go ahead and verify that it indeed is. There is one more trigger. Will the student see this? It is here that we enter an uncertain region. It is my hope that some students will figure this out. But for those that don't, we need not lose hope. The situation is not static and can be modified. For students who have not been triggered by anything else, we can give them more information in the situation:

$$
(5, 12, 13),\ \ (9, 40, 41),\ \ (19, 180, 181)
$$

This seems to be good enough situation for the students to find themselves in. The trigger here is more obvious. To better illustrate the framework, let us look at another situation.

## Another situation: a spiral of semicircles

### Trigger-1

The initial trigger seems straightforward. How we can make a spiral using semicircles? The nice thing about this question is that it is open ended. This is one way to construct a spiral using semicircles:

  <img src="/assets/posts/2019-12-10-situations/spiral.svg" width="90%" height="90%" />
  <caption><center><b>A spiral of semicircles</b></center></caption>
  <br>
</figure>

The first arm of the spiral is some arbitrary semicircle. To keep things simple, let it be centered at the origin with unit radius. How do we draw the second arm? For the construction to look like a spiral, the next semicircle should begin  where the first ended. The spiral could either become larger or it could become smaller. On a whim, let us go with the smaller one. What should be the size of the second arm? Instead of specifying the radius, let us draw a semicircle with its free endpoint lying on the center of the first arm. A more detailed construction follows.

#### Construction

- The spiral starts at the point S(-1, 0).
- The first arm is a semicircle of radius of 1 with center at the origin and lies above the X-axis. 
- The second arm is a semicircle that begins where the first arm ended, which is $(1, 0)$ and ends in the center of the first arm, which is $(0, 0)$. It lies below the axis. 
- Successive arms are constructed in a similar manner, by joining the end of the previous arm to its center with a semicircle, while alternating between the upper and lower half-planes.
- The endpoint of the arms are labeled starting from $0$.

Now that we have a spiral, let us see if it triggers any other ideas.

### Trigger-2

The arms of the spiral become smaller and smaller with every turn. Can we compute the radius of any arm of the spiral? If $r_n$ is the $n^{th}$ arm of the spiral with $r_0 = 1$, what is the value of $r_n$? As each arm connects the endpoint of the previous arm with its center, the radius of the previous arm becomes the diameter of the new arm. So $2r_n = r_{n - 1}$ or $r_n = \frac{r_{n - 1}}{2}$. Together with $r_0 = 1$, we have:

$$
r_n = \cfrac{1}{2^n}
$$

Any more triggers? Yes!

### Trigger-3

As the arms of the spiral get smaller and smaller, the radius of the arms keep diminishing and tend to zero in the limit. This means that the $n^{th}$ arm of the spiral seems to be converging to some point on the $X$-axis. This trigger leads to the question: as the number of arms tend to infinity do they converge, if so at what point? To answer this question, we need to track the endpoint of the spiral. Let $e_n$ denote the $X$-coordinate of the endpoint of the $n^{th}$ arm. We have $e_0 = 1$. This gives us two recurrence relations:

$$
\begin{align*}
e_{n + 1} &= e_{n} +  (-1)^{n + 1} \cdot 2r_{n + 1}\\
&= e_n + \cfrac{(-1)^{n + 1}}{2^{n}}\\
e_{n + 1} &= \cfrac{e_n + e_{n - 1}}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Assuming a limit exists, let us call it $L$. With just these equations, we cannot compute the limit. So we need to find a better relation, maybe a closed form expression for $e_n$, that will help us compute the limit. To aid us in this search, we shall compute the first few terms of $e_n$:<br><br>

| $n$ | $e_n$ |
| :---: | :---: |
| $0$ | $1$ |
| $1$ | $0$ |
| $2$ | $\cfrac{1}{2}$ |
| $3$ | $\cfrac{1}{4}$ |
| $4$ | $\cfrac{3}{8}$ |
| $5$ | $\cfrac{5}{16}$ |
| $6$ | $\cfrac{11}{32}$ |

<br><br>

Looking at the denominators of $e_n$, we see that it is a power of $2$. If this trend were to continue, the denominator of $e_n$ would be $2^{n-1}$. Also, the numerator seems to be an odd number. So the fraction is in its lowest terms. Going with these two observations, let us assume that the following statement holds for all integers $n$ less than $m$: 

$$
\text{Claim: }\ e_n = \cfrac{a_n}{2^{n-1}}\ \ \text{ and } a_n\ \text{ is odd}
$$

We are using the principle of strong induction here. The base case has already been established. Now for the inductive step:


$$
\begin{align*}
e_{m + 1} &= \cfrac{e_m + e_{m - 1}}{2}\\
&= \cfrac{1}{2} \cdot  \Bigg( \cfrac{a_{m}}{2^{m-1}} + \cfrac{a_{m-1}}{2^{m-2}} \Bigg)\\
&= \cfrac{a_{m}}{2^{m}}  + \cfrac{a_{m-1}}{2^{m-1}}\\
&= \cfrac{a_{m} + 2a_{m - 1}}{2^{m}}\\
\end{align*}
$$

$a_m$ and $a_{m - 1}$ are odd, so is $a_m + 2a_{m - 1}$. This proves our claim for all $n$. Let us rechristen the numerator of $e_{m + 1}$ to $a_{m + 1}$. This gives us another recurrence relation:

$$
a_{n + 1} = a_n + 2 a_{n - 1}
$$

 Now let us try to observe what $a_n$ looks like for the first few terms:<br><br>

| $n$ | $a_n$ |
| :---: | :---: |
| $0$ | $1$ |
| $1$ | $0$ |
| $2$ | $1$ |
| $3$ | $1$ |
| $4$ | $3$ |
| $5$ | $5$ |
| $6$ | $11$|
| $7$ | $21$ |

<br><br>

As we are multiplying the previous term by $2$ every time, let us see if there is a power of $2$ lurking anywhere in the general term of $a_n$. To that end, observe the following:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_0 + a_1 &= 1\\
a_1 + a_2 &= 1\\
a_2 + a_3 &= 2\\
a_3 + a_4 &= 4\\
a_4 + a_5 &= 8\\
a_5 + a_6 &= 16\\
a_6 + a_7 &= 32
\end{align*}
$$

These are powers of $2$. Going back to the recurrence relation, we get:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_{n + 1} &= a_n + 2a_{n - 1}\\
\implies a_{n + 1} + a_n &= 2(a_n + a_{n - 1})
\end{align*}
$$

Observe that $a_1 + a_2 = 1$, a power of $2$. And if $a_{n-1} + a_{n}$ is of the form $2^k$, $a_n + a_{n + 1} = 2^{k + 1}$. Again induction comes to the rescue. So we have:

$$
a_{n - 1} + a_{n} = 2^{n - 2}, \ \ \ n \geq 2
$$

With this we go back to $e_n$:

$$
\begin{align*}
a_{n - 1} + a_{n} &= 2^{n - 2}\\
\cfrac{a_{n - 1}}{2^{n-2}} + \cfrac{a_n}{2^{n-2}} &= 1\\
e_{n - 1} + 2e_n &= 1\\
\implies e_n &= \cfrac{1 - e_{n - 1}}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Now back to the limit. If we let $n \rightarrow \infty$:

$$
L = \cfrac{1 - L}{2} \implies L = \cfrac{1}{3}
$$

Are we done? No! <br><br>

All the while we assumed that a limit exists since it was visually obvious. But we still need to establish this fact. For a given integer $m$, let $\Bigg\| e_{m} - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg\| = \epsilon$. This measures the closeness of the endpoint from the point $(\frac{1}{3}, 0)$. Let us see how close the next endpoint of the spiral is to this point:

$$
\begin{align*}
\Bigg| e_{m + 1} - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg| &= \Bigg| \cfrac{1 - e_m}{2} - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg|\\
&= \Bigg| \cfrac{1}{6} - \cfrac{e_m}{2} \Bigg|\\
&= \cfrac{1}{2} \Bigg| e_m - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg|\\
&= \cfrac{\epsilon}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

As $\Bigg\|e_1 - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg\| = \cfrac{1}{3}$, we have:

$$
\Bigg| e_{n} - \cfrac{1}{3} \Bigg| = \cfrac{1}{3 \cdot 2^{n - 1}}, \ \ \ n \geq 1
$$

It is clear that the endpoint of the $n^{th}$ arm gets closer and closer to the point $(\frac{1}{3}, 0)$. Hence the limit exists and is $(\frac{1}{3}, 0)$. In some sense, the spiral has its eye at this limit point. With that shall we close this section? Has everything worth discovering been discovered? Again, no! We can ask few more questions. 

### Trigger-4

What is the perimeter of the spiral? Let us go ahead and compute it. As each arm is a semicircle, its perimeter is $\pi r_n$. So the perimeter of the spiral is this infinite sum:

$$
\begin{align*}
P &= \pi \cdot r_0 + \pi \cdot r_1 + \cdots + \pi \cdot r_n + \cdots \infty\\
&= \pi \Bigg(\cfrac{1}{2^0} + \cfrac{1}{2^1} + \cfrac{1}{2^2} + \cdots + \cfrac{1}{2^n} + \cdots \infty  \Bigg)\\
&= \pi \cfrac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{2}}\\
&= 2 \pi 
\end{align*}
$$

Note that the radius of the first semicircle is $1$. This means that the perimeter of the spiral is equal to the perimeter of a circle whose radius equals the radius of the first arm. In other words, if we think about the spiral being composed of an infinite thread and unravel it, there will be just enough thread to complete the circle corresponding to the first arm!<br><br>

Now what? 

### Trigger-5

We can also think about the area between the arms of the spiral. Think about a path created by the arms of a spiral: the first leg of this journey is bound by the arms $0$ and $2$ of the spiral, the next leg by arms $1$ and $3$, and so on. Above the $X$-axis, consecutive even arms form the path, while below the $X$-axis, consecutive odd arms form the path. If we decide to paint the path as we go along, what quantity of paint would be required?


<figure>  
  <img src="/assets/posts/2019-12-10-situations/spiral-area.svg" width="90%" height="90%" />
  <caption><center><b>The infinite road</b></center></caption>
  <br>
</figure>

Here is a table giving the areas of the first few legs of the journey along the road:<br><br>

| $n$ | $A_n$ |
| :---: | :---: |
| $0$ | $ \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_0^2 - r_2^2)$ |
| $1$ | $ \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_1^2 - r_3^2)$ |
| $2$ | $ \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_2^2 - r_4^2)$ |
| $3$ | $ \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_3^2 - r_5^2)$ |

<br><br>

Summing till infinity, we get:

$$
\begin{align*}
A &= \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_0^2 - r_2^2) + \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_1^2 - r_3^2) + \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_2^2 - r_4^2) + \cfrac{\pi}{2} (r_3^2 - r_5^2) + \cdots \infty\\
&= \cfrac{\pi}{2}(r_0^2 + r_1^2)
\end{align*}
$$

This is a telescoping sum and we have used the fact that $r_n \rightarrow 0$ as $n \rightarrow \infty$. Interestingly, this is the area bound between the first two arms and the $X$-axis. This does make sense as the entire spiral is contained in these two arms.



## Situations as a pedagogical tool

Teachers teach. They talk in front of the class for the most part and also use a board or slides as aids in teaching. A more polished term for all of this would be communication. Teachers communicate ideas to students. We could say that there is a transfer of ideas from the mind of the teacher to the mind of the student. This is the banking model of education as explained by Paulo Freire in his Pedagogy of the Oppressed. But how can the teacher share the joy of discovery? She can talk about one of her recent discoveries. But will the students be able to relate to it? Even if they understand most of what is being spoken, will the joy be recreated in them anew? That fleeting moment of discovery, the revelation of bumping into something unexpected, can that experience be transferred by traditional instruction? It is a question for which I don't have an answer.<br><br>

The framework of mathematical situations is an experiment that can shed light on this question. If students start discovering mathematical ideas by bumping into them with minimal help from the teacher, will the quality of learning improve? I feel positive about it. Note that the role of a teacher in this case becomes significantly different. There is still instruction along the traditional lines, but its prominence is greatly reduced. The situation must be chosen in such a way that it is at par with the student's level of mathematical maturity. In the best case scenario, the only communication from the teacher's side will be giving the student the mathematical situation. The rest of the process would be a slow unraveling of triggers, questions, problems and solutions on the student's side. If this happens, then the student is actually discovering mathematics. There could be no greater joy than that for both student and teacher!
