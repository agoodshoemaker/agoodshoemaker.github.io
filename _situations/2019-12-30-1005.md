The two chords subtend the angle $\angle A$ at the circumference. We have the well known result that $\angle BOC = 2 \angle A$. To a teacher and to a student who is familiar with circles, this result is an obvious trigger. Whereas for younger students, angles $\angle A$ and $\angle BOC$ may not seem particularly significant. In such cases, the teacher has to step in and modify the original situation so that an easier and more natural one is presented to the student. Here she should draw the student to some observation that kindles his interest which at the same time starts from some knowledge that he has already mastered. Say, the corollary of the above fact: the angle in a semicircle is a right angle. The modified situation would then be:

> A point on the circumference of a semicircle.


<figure>
	<img src="/assets/situations/situation-5/i2.svg" width="95%" height="95%" />
	<br>
</figure>




The teacher could ask students to pick some arbitrary point on the circumference of a semicircle and join this point to the endpoints of the diameter, and then measure the angle formed at the point by these two chords. Almost all of them see that this angle is pretty close to $90^{\circ}$. This provides an empirical observations for the students to form a hypothesis. This is particularly fruitful when students are working in a group. Each one would have  to choose a different point, but all would arrive at the same angle. This would come as a great surprise to students who have begun to respond to beauty in mathematical patterns.

> The teacher must try her best to withhold the statement of the theorem or result she wishes the student to master. The statement must emerge as a conjecture or hypothesis to explain a surprising observation. This way the habit of conjecturing and hypothesizing can be developed in the student.

Otherwise what happens is that the students never learn the art of forming conjectures from observations. The teacher is taken to be a machine that bombards students with problems whose solution is to be provided by the them. In the worst case - which is the situation in schools nowadays - the solution is also provided by the teacher and students are passive vessels into which the whole syllabus is dumped day after day.<br><br>

Another error that we commonly indulge in is to have a staple of results as theory and then provide a collection of problems that demonstrate the application of this theory. For example, in circles, we have about four to five theorems that discuss the properties of the angle subtended by a chord on the circumference. These become the theory and then there is a collection of practice problems that develop the student's fluency in applying the theory. Consider the following words of Dr. R.D. Sharma in his preface to one of his textbooks:

> It is my sincere advice to the students that in each chapter first they should go through the **theory and concepts** thoroughly, then they should attempt to solve the **illustrative examples** without looking at their solutions. Students should consult the solutions only when they are unable to solve on their own.


The second part of the advice is sound in principle and we have just alluded to it in the earlier paragraphs. I have a problem with the first part of it.

<br><br>

With this observational trigger, let us see how one can go about proving the conjecture:

## Trigger-1

Points interact with each other by means of a line segment. So let us go ahead and join $P$ with $A$, $B$ and $O$.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/situations/situation-5/i3.svg" width="95%" height="95%" />
	<br>
</figure>

We get two isosceles triangles: $OAP$ and $OBP$. This means we could do something with the angles. Let us call the equal angles in either triangle $\angle 1$ and $\angle 2$. 

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/situations/situation-5/i4.svg" width="95%" height="95%" />
	<br>
</figure>

Now, we can just use the angle sum property to get:

$$
\begin{align*}
2 \angle 1 + \angle POA = 180^{\circ}\\
2 \angle 2 + \angle POB = 180^{\circ}
\end{align*}
$$

We also note that $AB$ is a straight line, implying $\angle POA + \angle POB = 180^{\circ}$. So there is some benefit in adding the above equations, which results in:

$$
\begin{align*}
2 (\angle 1 + \angle 2) + (\angle POA + \angle POB) &= 360^{\circ}\\
\implies 2 (\angle 1 + \angle 2) + 180^{\circ} &= 360^{\circ}\\
\implies 2 (\angle 1 + \angle 2) &= 180^{\circ}\\
\implies \angle 1 + \angle 2 &= 90^{\circ}\\
\implies \angle APB &= 90^{\circ}
\end{align*}
$$

Lo and behold! The angle at the point $P$ is indeed a right angle. The other way this could have been done is to start with a right triangle and then draw a circle with the hypotenuse as a diameter. Students would be surprised to find that the circle passes through the third vertex. In fact this could be used to motivate the converse of the above result.
