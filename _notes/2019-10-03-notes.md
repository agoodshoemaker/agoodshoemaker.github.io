## [mathematics; algebra; factorization]


### What is factorization?

Consider the following problem:<br><br>

> You are given a certain number of squares of size $1 \times 1$. The exact number is not revealed to you, but there are integers $x$ and $y$ such that the number of squares is equal to the expression: $xy + x + y + 1$. Can these squares be arranged so as to form a rectangle?

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/notes/2019-10-03/i1.png" width="65%" height="65%" />
	<figcaption>Squares</figcaption>
	<br>
</figure>

We have squares all over the place. Let us call them free squares. Observe that the free squares are indistinguishable as there is nothing unique about any particular square. This is the visual world. In the algebraic world, we have the expression for the number of squares: $xy + x + y + 1$. Now let us begin to group the squares in the visual world taking the help of the expression from the algebraic world. As we want to build a rectangle, let us begin with one that is already present in the expression, namely, $xy$. This is a rectangle made up of $x$ rows, each row having $y$ squares. The total number of squares is $xy$. Note that we could also view it as a rectangle made of $y$ columns with $x$ squares in each column.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/notes/2019-10-03/i2.png" width="65%" height="65%" />
	<figcaption>A rectangle and some squares</figcaption>
	<br>
</figure>

Now that we have placed $xy$ squares firmly in a rectangular configuration, the number of free squares that are still floating in the visual world is equal to: $x + y + 1$. Since our constructed rectangle has $x$ rows and $y$ columns, let us take $x$ free squares and add one more column. We obtain a new rectangle of sides $x$ and $y + 1$. This is another way of saying that there are $x$ rows and $y + 1$ columns. This leaves us with $x + 1$ squares floating in the free space.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/notes/2019-10-03/i3.png" width="65%" height="65%" />
	<figcaption>A bigger rectangle in the making</figcaption>
	<br>
</figure>

Continuing this process, let us add the $x$ squares to form a new row on the top. This results in a rectangle with a hole in it. It is one square short. The new rectangle we are forming has $y + 1$ columns and $x + 1$ rows, one more than the previous one, but it is also one square short. Thankfully we have one free square left.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/notes/2019-10-03/i4.png" width="65%" height="65%" />
	<figcaption>Almost there</figcaption>
	<br>
</figure>

We take the last free square and plug the hole in the rectangle. This new rectangle has $x + 1$ rows and $y + 1$ columns.

<figure>
	<img src="/assets/notes/2019-10-03/i5.png" width="65%" height="65%" />
	<figcaption>The Rectangle!</figcaption>
</figure>

We started off with $xy + x + y + 1$ free squares and have used all of them to build a rectangle of size $(x + 1) \times (y + 1)$. Algebraically, we express this as:

> $$
xy + x + y + 1 = (x + 1)(y + 1)
$$


Factorization is a process that converts an algebraic expression into a compact product of smaller expressions. It is akin to making ice from water by cooling it. Factorization is the reverse of multiplication. Factorization is constructive, multiplication is destructive. Multiplication tears down boundaries, factorization erects boundaries. When two binomials are multiplied, troops from one binomial invade the other, one at a time, each one breaking the walls that separate them. This simple analogy explains why multiplication is trivial but factorization is non-trivial. It is easy to destroy, difficult to build. A great building can be razed to the ground in seconds, it takes several months if not years to construct one. Multiplication breaks, factorization groups. Multiplication diffuses individual identities, factorization crystallizes them. Thankfully, factorization-multiplication is a reversible process.

>  $$
(x + 1)(y + 1)\ \  \underset{Factorization}{\stackrel{Multiplication}{\rightleftharpoons}}\ \  xy + x + y + 1
$$

There are different tools, techniques and methods employed in constructing buildings. Same is the case with factorization. For a classification of the different techniques, refer these <a href="/notes/2019-09-23-notes.html" target="blank">notes</a>.<br><br>

Computationally, a factored expression is cheaper to perform. What does this statement mean? Let us plug in values for these numbers. Let us say $x = 999999$ and $y = 9999$. Now consider the product:

$$
999999 \times 9999 + 999999 + 9999 + 1
$$

If we are to evaluate this expression as it is, we will have to make one multiplications and three additions, a total of four computational operations. But let us first factorize this before evaluating it:

$$
(999999 + 1)(9999 + 1) = 10^7 \times 10^5 = 10^{12}
$$

Here we had to do two additions and one multiplication, a total of three computational operations, which is cheaper than the previous case. Here the factored expressions also turned out to be far simpler. But even otherwise, we need to note that multiplying $x + 1$ and $y + 1$ is not going to be all that more difficult than multiplying $x$ and $y$. So there certainly is a reduction in computational effort.
