When we draw a diagram corresponding to a geometric statement, we are making a transition from the world of abstract ideas to physical reality. Once we make this transition, there is a tendency to remain in the physical reality because that is closer to us and is more relatable. This is where we err. The diagram is a visual support to help us reason about abstract ideas, not an exact representation of mathematical truth. The ideal way is to keep going back and forth between ideas and diagrams, use the diagram for directions, but rely on rigorous reasoning with ideas to seal the deal. What expains our gullibility in drawing wrong inferences from diagrams? The sense of vision in humans is much more developed than the capacity of logical reasoning which is still in its nascent stage of develpment. In fact, geometry is one of the first arenas for the development of this faculty.

This may seem naïve.
