## Textbook

> The teacher must try her best to withhold the statement of the theorem or result she wishes the student to master. Instead, the statement must emerge in the student's mind as a conjecture or hypothesis to explain a surprising observation. This way the habit of conjecturing and hypothesizing can be developed in the student.

Otherwise what happens is that the students never learn the art of forming conjectures from observations. The teacher is taken to be a machine that bombards students with problems whose solution is to be provided by the them. In the worst case - which is the situation in schools nowadays - the solution is also provided by the teacher and students are passive vessels into which the whole syllabus is dumped in day after day.<br><br>

Another error that we commonly indulge in is to have a more or less fixed staple of mathematical results that we term theory immediately followed by a collection of problems that demonstrate the application of this theory. For example, in the chapter dealing with circles, we have about four to five theorems that lay down the properties of the angle subtended by a chord on the circumference. These theorems become the theory; then there is a set of practice problems that develop the student's fluency in applying this theory. The issue with this approach is that the theorems drop onto the student's heads like rain on a sunny day. There is no context surrounding it, the theorem or result is not progressively built from earlier observations, it doesn't emerge organically. Its introduction is so sudden and out of context that there is an air of artificiality about it.<br><br>


Consider the following words of Dr. R.D. Sharma in the preface to one of his textbooks:

> It is my sincere advice to the students that in each chapter first they should go through the **theory and concepts** thoroughly, then they should attempt to solve the **illustrative examples** without looking at their solutions. Students should consult the solutions only when they are unable to solve on their own.


This is the traditional approach. At this stage there are some questions worth asking:

1. How can a topic be treated from the beginning to the end without brining in the demarcation of theory-application?
2. If such a treatment were to be possible, in what respects does it differ from the traditional approach?
3. What are the advantages and disadvantages of such an approach?

A renascence should focus on several aspects. It need not result in a complete overhaul of the existing system. But every aspect of it must be questioned, challenged and debated:

- Problems
	- new problems
	- re-framing old problems by brining out the aesthetic element hidden in problems
	- infuse other novel elements like the history of mathematics and mathematicians into the problems
	- eliminate problem-smell
- Curriculum
	- widen the range of topics study
	- increase the depth of content covered in each topic
- Pedagogy
	- explore new ways to present mathematics
	- technology in mathematics
- Testing
	- exams
	- MCQs and their impact
	- better ways of testing - different question types
- Learning
	- fluency versus understanding
	- long term retention
	- place for proofs in high school mathematics
